The Future of Living, Working, and Healing

La Ruche is a premium, integrated living environment that brings together housing, behavioral health, wellness, and work into a single, purpose-built ecosystem. In West Hollywood, the model combines residential co-living, outpatient clinical services, clinical coworking, and a curated wellness platform—including longevity, recovery, and performance offerings—to support people across every stage of health and life. By aligning the environment, community, and care into a single asset, La Ruche replaces fragmented, episodic models with a continuous, experience-driven approach that improves outcomes while generating multiple complementary revenue streams. It is not a treatment center or a coworking space, but a new category of real estate designed for living well, working effectively, and sustaining long-term recovery and performance.

Why build all four at once?
Lower cost to acquire customers
A treatment patient becomes a co-living resident becomes a club member. You only paid to find them once.
Better outcomes = better business
When patients live and socialize in the same ecosystem, recovery rates improve — which drives referrals and reputation.
If one revenue stream slows, others don't
An insurance cut doesn't affect apartment rents. A slow membership month doesn't affect clinical revenue. Diversification protects the whole.

Where the money goes

Every dollar coming in has to cover costs before anything is profit. Here are the major expense categories — and why the model is specifically designed to keep costs flexible rather than fixed.

Imagine a catering company. Fixed costs are the kitchen you rent and the salaried chef — you pay those no matter how many events you book. Variable costs are ingredients — you only buy them when someone hires you. La Ruche is engineered to have mostly variable costs in the clinical business, so if occupancy drops 30%, costs drop nearly 30% too. That's the key financial design decision.

Why "variable" clinical costs matter
The therapists and clinicians at La Ruche are contracted per patient — not full-time salaried employees. That means:

• At 50% patient capacity → clinical staffing costs drop ~50%
• At 100% capacity → full costs, but full revenue
• No heavy fixed payroll to carry through a slow month

This is specifically called a "privileged provider marketplace" model in the business plan. It's the single most important cost management decision in the whole structure.
Key financial engineering in the model
The one cost that never changes
Debt service — the monthly mortgage payment on the $49.5M loan — is fixed at approximately $3.0M per year regardless of revenue. This is the floor that every other part of the business must clear before any profit reaches investors.

In Year 1 (ramp-up), this fixed cost weighs heavily against modest revenue. By Year 3, when the building is full, it represents only about 10% of total revenue.
Biggest risk: revenue drops faster than variable costs can shrink, leaving insufficient cash to cover the loan.

What's actually left over

Finance uses multiple overlapping terms for "profit" that each measure something slightly different. Here's a plain-English guide to the four key terms used in this model.

EBITDA  $11.5M at Year 3
Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortization
38% margin
$11.5M out of every $29.95M of revenue
Think of it as your paycheck before taxes are taken out. It measures the cash profit from actually running the business — before accounting for the loan payments, taxes, or accounting write-downs. Investors use this number to compare businesses across different tax situations and financing structures.
NOI — Net Operating Income  $7.0M (PropCo only)
The real estate version of profit
78% margin
$7.0M out of $9.0M in rents collected
This measures what the building itself earns from rents, after paying property taxes, insurance, and maintenance — but before the mortgage. Banks and real estate buyers use NOI to decide what a building is worth. The famous rule: Value = NOI ÷ cap rate. So $7.0M NOI at a 6% cap rate implies the building is worth ~$117M.
Free Cash Flow  $8.5M at Year 3
What's left after paying the mortgage
This is the money actually available to pay investors, reinvest in the business, or distribute as dividends. Year 1 is very tight (~$500K) because the building is ramping up and revenue is low while the mortgage payment is constant. By Year 3, free cash flow opens up dramatically — $8.5M — because revenue has scaled but debt service stayed the same.

What the whole thing is worth

There are two completely separate ways to value La Ruche — one for the real estate, one for the operating businesses. Most of the confusion in the original business plan came from mixing these up.

Imagine you own a McDonald's franchise in a prime location. The land and building are worth something on their own — you could sell just the real estate to a developer. But the franchise business (the brand, the customers, the cash flow from selling burgers) is also worth something separately. Someone might pay a premium for both together. La Ruche has exactly this two-layer structure.
Layer 1 — The real estate (PropCo)
Real estate investors measure value using a "cap rate" — the annual return they expect. In West Hollywood, investors typically accept 5.5–6.0% annual returns on premium mixed-use assets.

The three assumptions that matter most
Drag these three sliders down and watch what breaks first.
1
Clinical occupancy
The clinic is 63% of total revenue. If occupancy stays below 70% — which can happen during early licensing, slow referral networks, or insurance denials — free cash flow turns negative. The business doesn't fail, but it stops covering its debt. This is the single biggest risk in the entire model. Drag the "Clinical occupancy rate" slider to 65% and watch the DSCR fall below 1.25×.
2
Average daily rate from insurance
Health insurance companies negotiate rates down every year. The model assumes $750/day. If insurance companies push that to $500/day — which is realistic for Medi-Cal or lower-tier plans — clinical revenue falls by $5.6M, wiping out almost the entire profit margin. Private-pay patients at $1,000+/day are the premium that protects the model.
3
Time to stabilization
Every month of delay in getting clinically licensed or reaching full building occupancy costs approximately $500K in projected revenue — while the $3M/year debt service keeps running. A 6-month slip in the licensing timeline (not unusual in California) adds $3M in cumulative cash burn before the first clinical dollar is earned. The model assumes 18–24 months from renovation completion to clinical stabilization.
